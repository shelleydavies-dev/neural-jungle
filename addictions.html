The Looping Vines

Addictions

The trails are deep, worn into the earth by repetition. The vine reaches for your hand before you've decided to reach for it.

Addiction is not a failure of willpower. It is a hijacking of the brain's oldest learning system.

Addiction rewires your brain's reward system. The nucleus accumbens, a small region deep in the brain, learns to prioritise an addictive substance above everything else — food, safety, relationships. This isn't a choice. It's a neurological hijacking of the system your brain uses to decide what matters most.

Dopamine is not the pleasure chemical — it is the wanting chemical. The molecule of craving and prediction. The basal ganglia wear the trail so deep that walking it requires no thought. Leaving it requires everything.

Dopamine doesn't create pleasure — it creates wanting. It's the brain's way of saying "do that again, it's important." Addictive drugs flood the brain with 3-10x more dopamine than natural rewards. Over time, the brain reduces its own dopamine receptors, so you need more just to feel normal. Meanwhile, the behaviour becomes automatic — handled by the basal ganglia (habit centre), bypassing the prefrontal cortex (decision-making).

Psilocybin-assisted therapy achieved 80% tobacco abstinence at six months. The person sees the loop from above it for the first time.

You cannot think your way out of a loop. You have to see it from above.

Psilocybin disrupts the rigid automatic patterns of addiction. Brain imaging shows it restores activity in the prefrontal cortex (your decision-making centre) while reducing the dominance of habit circuits. At Johns Hopkins, 80% of smokers were abstinent after 6 months of psilocybin therapy — more than double the best conventional treatment. The key seems to be gaining perspective on the pattern from outside it.
