Psychedelics for Depression & Anxiety

What the research actually shows — the promising findings, the honest limitations, and what it means for the trails in your neural landscape that have gone dark and dormant.

The Deep Rut

Depression isn’t always sadness. Often it’s numbness — a dormant season of the mind where everything loses its color. Food, music, people, ideas: they register, but they don’t land. The undergrowth has gone still. Alongside that numbness, or sometimes beneath it, is rumination — the brain walking the same dark trail over and over. The default mode network (DMN), the region most active during self-referential thought, becomes hyperconnected. One trail becomes so deeply worn it’s a trench. The canopy closes overhead. The world goes flat.

Standard treatments — SSRIs, CBT, talk therapy — work by gradually widening the trail or teaching you to notice when you’re caught in the rut. They help many people. But for roughly 30% of those with depression, conventional treatments don’t provide adequate relief. These are the people researchers increasingly call “treatment-resistant” — and they’re the ones driving the search for alternatives.

What the Research Has Found

Psilocybin for Depression

Several landmark clinical trials have examined psilocybin-assisted therapy for depression. In these studies, participants receive one or two carefully dosed sessions of psilocybin alongside preparation and integration therapy — not a pill they take home. The therapy context is considered essential.

The results have been notable. Studies from Johns Hopkins (2022) and Imperial College London (2021) found that psilocybin-assisted therapy produced rapid and significant reductions in depression symptoms, with effects lasting weeks to months after just one or two sessions. Some participants in the Johns Hopkins trial maintained improvement at 12-month follow-up.

What’s happening in the brain: Psilocybin appears to temporarily quiet the default mode network — the brain’s most worn trail goes still. The rumination loop pauses. And in that clearing, the brain forms new connections — like sunlight reaching the forest floor for the first time in years, waking dormant seeds. It’s not that new pathways are invented. It’s that pathways the undergrowth had buried are suddenly accessible. Many participants describe the return of something they thought was permanently lost: the ability to feel. To be moved. To experience awe — like color returning after a long, grey winter.

What We Still Don’t Know

Honesty about limitations is as important as reporting the findings. Here’s what the science hasn’t resolved yet:

Long-term efficacy. Most studies have followed participants for months, not years. We don’t yet know how lasting the benefits are, or how often booster sessions might be needed.

Who it works for. Not everyone responds to psychedelic-assisted therapy. We don’t yet have reliable predictors of who will benefit most.

The therapy vs. the substance. Clinical trials pair the substance with extensive therapy. We don’t know how much of the benefit comes from the substance itself versus the therapeutic relationship, the setting, and the expectation.

Risks for specific populations. People with bipolar disorder, psychotic disorders, or certain cardiac conditions have generally been excluded from trials. The evidence for these groups is thin to nonexistent.

Psilocybin vs. SSRIs

One of the most common questions we receive. A 2021 trial from Imperial College London directly compared psilocybin-assisted therapy with escitalopram (a common SSRI) over six weeks. Both groups showed improvements, with no statistically significant difference on the primary outcome measure. Psilocybin showed advantages on several secondary measures, including general wellbeing and the ability to feel emotions.

The takeaway isn’t that one is “better.” They appear to work through different mechanisms — SSRIs gradually adjust the chemical baseline over weeks; psilocybin creates a concentrated window of psychological flexibility. SSRIs are like slowly pruning an overgrown trail over weeks. Psilocybin is more like a controlled burn — a brief, intense clearing that lets new growth emerge, but requires careful management and integration.

If you’re currently on SSRIs: Do not stop taking prescribed medication to try psychedelics. SSRIs can interact with psilocybin (potentially reducing effects) and can be dangerous to stop abruptly. Any change to your treatment plan should be discussed with your prescribing physician.

Practical Next Steps

If you’re interested in exploring psychedelic-assisted therapy, here are the evidence-based trails forward:

Clinical trials. ClinicalTrials.gov lists ongoing studies recruiting participants for psilocybin and MDMA-assisted therapy across the country.

Supervised psilocybin in Oregon and Colorado. Both states have implemented frameworks for supervised psilocybin use with trained facilitators.

Talk to your provider. Bring the research. A growing number of psychiatrists and therapists are informed about psychedelic-assisted therapy and can help you evaluate whether it’s appropriate for your situation.

Important: This content is for educational purposes only and is not medical advice. Psychedelics carry real risks and are not appropriate for everyone. Always consult a qualified healthcare provider before making any decisions about psychedelic use.
