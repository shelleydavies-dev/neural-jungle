Prefrontal Cortex

The Sunken Grove

Depression

The canopy has closed completely. Not a single fracture of light. It is a heaviness — a gravitational pull downward that looks like laziness from the outside but feels like being buried alive in slow motion.

Depression is not a darkness you carry. It is a darkness that carries you.

Depression isn't just "feeling sad." It's a cascade of changes across the brain. The prefrontal cortex (planning, motivation, decision-making) becomes underactive, while the amygdala (threat detection) becomes overactive. You feel the danger but can't find the exit. It's like your brain's navigation system going dark while its alarm system runs on overdrive.

It is not a chemical imbalance — it is an ecosystem collapse. The prefrontal cortex dims. Neuroplasticity drops. Dendrites physically shrink. The roots harden and new growth stops. This is why depression feels so fixed — because at the cellular level, it is.

Depression reduces neuroplasticity — the brain's ability to form new connections and adapt. A protein called BDNF, essential for neural growth, drops significantly. The dendrites (branching extensions of brain cells) physically shrink, especially in the prefrontal cortex and hippocampus. Meanwhile, inflammation increases in the brain. The result: the brain becomes rigid, stuck, unable to respond to new information or form new patterns.

Ketamine produces antidepressant effects within hours. Psilocybin promotes the literal regrowth of neural branches that depression had pruned. The hardened roots soften. The grove flexes.

The light doesn't come from above. It comes from the roots remembering how to grow.

Ketamine triggers a rapid burst of glutamate that jump-starts neuroplasticity within hours — compared to weeks for traditional antidepressants. Psilocybin goes even further: research shows it promotes actual dendritic growth — the physical regrowth of neural branches. Brain regions that depression had isolated start communicating again. In a 2021 trial, psilocybin matched the leading SSRI in 6 weeks, with faster onset and fewer side effects.
