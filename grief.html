The Weeping Canopy

Grief

The canopy closes. The rain begins. Your nervous system doesn't distinguish between types of loss — it responds the same way: the alarm vines fire, the flood rises, and the roots of attachment are violently exposed.

Grief is not a problem to be solved. It is a landscape to be crossed.

When you lose someone close to you, your brain's threat-detection centre (the amygdala) triggers a full stress response — as if you're in physical danger. Cortisol, the stress hormone, floods your system. This is why grief can feel like panic, exhaustion, and physical pain all at once.

The brain builds dedicated circuits for our closest attachments. When someone is gone, those circuits keep firing — searching, predicting their return. Grief is the brain slowly, painfully updating its model of reality.

Your brain has neural pathways specifically wired to the people you love. When they're gone, those pathways don't just switch off — they keep expecting the person to return. The nucleus accumbens (the same area involved in craving) keeps predicting their presence. Grief is your brain gradually learning a new reality.

Psilocybin quiets the default mode network — the brain's narrative engine that replays loss on loop. The rigid thought patterns soften. In clinical trials, a single dose allowed people to process grief that years of talk therapy had not unlocked.

The clearing doesn't erase the rain. It changes your relationship to it.

Psilocybin temporarily reduces activity in the default mode network (DMN) — the part of your brain that generates your internal monologue and replays memories. In grief, the DMN gets stuck in a loop of "what if" and "if only." Psilocybin loosens that loop, allowing the brain to process the loss from a new perspective. In NYU trials, one session produced significant reductions in prolonged grief.
