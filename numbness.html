Brain Stem

The Still Marsh

Numbness

The water is perfectly still. The air barely moves. This is not the sharp pain of grief but something more unsettling: the absence of feeling altogether.

Numbness is not the absence of feeling. It is feeling's bodyguard.

Numbness is your nervous system's oldest protection mechanism. When the brain decides that neither fighting nor fleeing will work, it activates the "freeze" response — shutting down emotion, slowing the heart, and withdrawing from sensation. You're not broken. Your brain is protecting you from overwhelm.

The dorsal vagal complex governs the freeze response. Heart rate slows, emotion flatlines, the marsh goes still. The insula — the brain's body-sensing centre — goes quiet. You literally lose the ability to feel what's happening inside you.

The dorsal vagal complex is the oldest branch of your autonomic nervous system (the system that runs automatically, like heartbeat and digestion). When activated, it slows everything down. The insula, which is the brain region responsible for sensing your internal body state, goes offline. This is why numbness feels like being disconnected from your own body — because you temporarily are.

MDMA creates a neurochemical window where feeling becomes possible again without triggering another shutdown. Under psilocybin, the insula wakes up. Participants report feeling their own heartbeat for the first time in years.

The fog lifts because the nervous system finally believes it's safe enough to feel.

MDMA reduces activity in the amygdala (fear centre) while boosting oxytocin and serotonin. This creates a safe window where you can start to feel emotions again without your brain re-triggering the freeze response. Psilocybin reactivates the insula, literally restoring your ability to sense your own body. Many participants in clinical trials describe reconnecting with physical sensation they hadn't felt in years.
