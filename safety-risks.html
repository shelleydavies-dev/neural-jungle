The Honest Guide to Psychedelic Safety

Every experienced guide knows: the wilderness is beautiful and it is dangerous. Respecting both truths is how you move through it safely. Here’s what you need to know.

Who Should Not Enter

Some trails are not safe for certain travelers. This isn’t gatekeeping — it’s the clearest data we have on where the ground gives way:

Personal or family history of psychosis or schizophrenia. Psychedelics can trigger or worsen psychotic episodes. This is the most consistently documented serious risk in the literature.

Bipolar disorder. Psychedelics may trigger manic episodes. Most clinical trials exclude participants with bipolar diagnoses.

Current lithium use. The combination of lithium and psychedelics has been associated with seizures. This is considered a hard contraindication.

Certain cardiac conditions. Serotonergic psychedelics can affect heart rate and blood pressure. People with significant cardiovascular conditions should consult a cardiologist.

Pregnancy. There is almost no research on psychedelic use during pregnancy. The precautionary principle applies strongly here.

Medication Interactions

Your neural landscape doesn’t exist in isolation — it’s already being shaped by whatever you’re taking. Some interactions are inconvenient; others are genuinely dangerous.

SSRIs and SNRIs may reduce or block the effects of psilocybin. Some people compensate by taking higher doses, which introduces additional risk. Never adjust prescribed medication without your doctor’s guidance.

MAO inhibitors can cause serotonin syndrome when combined with serotonergic psychedelics — a potentially life-threatening condition. This is especially relevant for ayahuasca, which contains a natural MAO inhibitor.

Tramadol lowers the seizure threshold and should not be combined with psychedelics.

Challenging Experiences

Not every journey through the wilderness is peaceful. Difficult psychedelic experiences are common and well-documented. Research indicates that roughly 14% of users report prolonged anxiety following use, and a subset report extended difficulties lasting weeks to months.

Common elements of challenging experiences include intense anxiety or panic, confrontation with painful emotions or memories, temporary loss of sense of self (ego dissolution) experienced as frightening rather than liberating, paranoid or confused thinking, and a sense that the experience will never end.

The wilderness perspective: A challenging experience is not necessarily a bad one — many people report that their most difficult psychedelic experiences were also their most transformative. But this reframing should never minimize the real distress people experience. The goal is preparation and support, not dismissal.

Harm Reduction Basics

If someone chooses to use psychedelics, these practices — drawn from clinical research and harm reduction organizations — significantly reduce risk:

Test your substances. Reagent testing kits can identify whether a substance is what it’s claimed to be. They don’t measure purity or potency, but they catch dangerous substitutions.

Start low. Especially with unfamiliar sources. You can always take more; you can never take less.

Have a trusted sitter. Someone sober who can provide calm reassurance if things get difficult. Clinical trials always include trained guides for a reason.

Choose your setting carefully. Familiar, comfortable, safe. Not a festival or party if it’s your first time. Not around people you don’t trust.

Clear your schedule. A full experience takes 4–12 hours depending on the substance, and you may feel tender or reflective for a day or two afterward. Don’t schedule anything demanding.

Integration afterward. The experience itself is only one part of the process. Talking through what happened with a therapist, integration circle, or trusted person helps you translate insights into lasting change.

If you’re in crisis: If you or someone you know is experiencing prolonged psychological distress following psychedelic use, the Fireside Project offers a psychedelic peer support line: 62-FIRESIDE (623-473-7433). For immediate danger, call 911.

Important: This content is for educational purposes only and is not medical advice. Psychedelics carry real risks and are not appropriate for everyone. Always consult a qualified healthcare provider before making any decisions about psychedelic use.
